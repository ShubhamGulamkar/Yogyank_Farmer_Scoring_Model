# Yogyank Scoring Engine — Audit Memo

**Reviewed by:** ML Engineering Audit  
**Date:** 2026-05-28  
**Assessment round:** Round 1 Technical Assessment

---

## Paragraph 1 — Why the original script was dangerous and the high validation score was not trustworthy

The original script contained five compounding flaws that made it unsuitable for any real scoring decision. The first and most serious was **data leakage**: the feature `defaulted_in_next_12_months` — a label that records whether a farmer actually defaulted *after* the application — was used as a model input. At real scoring time, that column does not yet exist. Including it essentially told the model the answer during training, making the reported R² of ~0.99 statistically meaningless. Any model with access to its own label will appear to predict perfectly; this inflates confidence without providing any genuine forecasting ability. The second flaw was **target contamination through policy mixing**: the script modified `target_entitlement_score` by subtracting 150 points for all farmers with `pm_kisan_status == "No"` *before* training. This silently baked a business-policy decision into the mathematical target, meaning the model was not learning a neutral entitlement signal — it was learning a pre-judged, policy-adjusted score. Separating those two concerns is not a style preference; it is a requirement for auditability, because a bank auditor or regulator needs to be able to inspect the model and the policy independently. The third flaw was **unsafe categorical encoding**: a single `LabelEncoder` instance was re-used across multiple columns, overwriting its internal state on each call. This means the encoding for `crop_type` was silently discarded when `pm_kisan_status` was encoded next. Nominal categories were assigned arbitrary integers that impose a false ordinal relationship (e.g. implying "Wheat > Cotton"). No encoding mappings were saved, so scoring new data would produce different numeric representations than training saw. The fourth flaw was **an unrealistic validation strategy**: `train_test_split` with `shuffle=True` randomly mixed records from 2022, 2023, and 2024 across train and test sets. In a real deployment the model is trained on historical cohorts and scored on a *future* cohort it has never seen. Shuffling allows the model to "borrow" 2024 patterns during training, producing optimistic metrics that do not reflect future performance. The fifth flaw was **no auditability**: the only saved artifact was the model file. There were no frozen encoders, no feature schema, no version stamp, and no reason-code mechanism. A saved model file in isolation cannot be reproduced, audited, or explained to a farmer whose credit application was affected.

---

## Paragraph 2 — What was changed and how it reduces risk

The rewritten script addresses each flaw in a targeted, documentable way. **Leakage** is eliminated by explicitly removing `defaulted_in_next_12_months` from the feature set and adding a runtime guard that logs its exclusion; the exclusion reason is also written to `feature_schema.json` so any future engineer can see why it was dropped. **Policy separation** is enforced by reverting the target back to its raw `target_entitlement_score` values and leaving `pm_kisan_status` as a plain input feature — exactly like `irrigation_type` or `soil_type`. Any bank-specific risk adjustment must happen downstream, in the versioned policy layer that is outside the model, not inside the training loop. **Encoding** is replaced with one `OrdinalEncoder` instance per categorical column, each fitted exclusively on training data and then frozen. Unseen categories at scoring time receive a sentinel value (`-1`) rather than crashing or silently re-learning. All encoder objects are serialised to `artifacts/encoders.pkl` alongside their learned category lists, so the exact same transformation is reproducible. **Validation** is redesigned as an out-of-time (OOT) split: 2022 and 2023 data train the model; 2024 data is the held-out test set. This mirrors real deployment, where the model will always be scored against a future cohort. **Auditability** is addressed through a four-artifact bundle saved to the `artifacts/` folder: the model (`xgboost_yogyank.pkl`), the frozen encoders (`encoders.pkl`), the feature schema (`feature_schema.json`, which lists every included feature, every excluded feature and its reason, and the train/test year split), and a version manifest (`version_manifest.json`) containing the version string, UTC training timestamp, OOT metrics, and MD5 checksums of all other artifacts. Finally, top-3 SHAP-based reason codes are computed for every farmer in the OOT set and saved to `oot_reason_codes.csv`, providing a concrete explainability trail.

## Limitations

**One thing I would not yet trust:** The OOT test set for 2024 is small (the synthetic sample has approximately 100–150 rows in that year). An R² computed on ~100 rows has wide confidence intervals; a single cluster of unusual farmers can swing it by 0.05 or more. I would not cite the OOT R² as a reliable performance estimate until the test set has at least 500 independent farmers, ideally from a genuinely held-out geographic region rather than just a later year within the same districts.

**One thing I would improve with more time:** The SHAP reason codes are computed on raw SHAP values, which reflect ordinal-encoded category numbers, not the original category names. I would add a reverse-mapping step so the reason code reads "crop_type = Sugarcane pushed score up by +42 points" rather than a numeric reference. I would also add a proper `scikit-learn` `Pipeline` object so that preprocessing and model inference are a single atomic unit — reducing the risk of a future engineer applying encoders in the wrong order or forgetting to apply them at all.

## Monitoring

After deployment, the two slices I would prioritise for ongoing fairness and stability monitoring are:

1. **`pm_kisan_status` (Yes / No)** — Because this feature was previously misused to directly penalise scores, it is the highest-risk axis for disparate impact. After deployment, I would track whether the mean predicted score and score-to-approval conversion rate differ materially between the two groups, controlling for income and repayment history. A statistically significant gap that cannot be explained by legitimate financial risk signals would warrant a re-audit.

2. **`crop_type` × `district` interaction** — Seasonal crop failures and regional weather shocks affect specific crop-district combinations asymmetrically. If a drought hits Ganjam Rice farmers in a scoring cohort, their `rainfall_deviation_pct` and `ndvi_score` will shift out of the training distribution. Monitoring score distribution drift and MAE by this intersection would surface model degradation early, before it affects credit decisions at scale. Additional slices worth tracking over time include `irrigation_type` (Rainfed vs Irrigated is a strong proxy for climate vulnerability) and `landholding_band` (small marginal farmers below 2 acres are under-represented in most training samples and are the group most harmed by mis-scoring).
